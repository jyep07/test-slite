# Black Holes

A black hole is a region of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing —
not even light — can escape from it.

## Overview

Black holes originate from when enormous objects collapse under their own gravity. The
boundary beyond which nothing can return is called the **event horizon**. At the
center lies the **singularity**, a point of theoretically infinite density.

## Types of Black Holes

| Type | Mass range | Origin |
|------|------------|--------|
| Stellar-mass | ~3–100 solar masses | Collapse of a massive star |
| Intermediate-mass | 100–100,000 solar masses | Mergers / uncertain |
| Supermassive | Millions to billions of solar masses | Centers of galaxies |

## Key Concepts

- **Event horizon** — the point of no return.
- **Accretion disk** — superheated matter spiraling inward, emitting intense
  radiation.
- **Hawking radiation** — a theoretical process by which black holes slowly
  lose mass and energy over time.
- **Spaghettification** — the stretching of objects by extreme tidal forces
  near a black hole.

## Milestones

- **2015** — LIGO detected gravitational waves from two merging black holes.
- **2019** — The Event Horizon Telescope captured the first image of a black
  hole, in galaxy M87.
- **2022** — The first image of Sagittarius A*, the Milky Way's central black
  hole, was released.
